Hair Structure & Keratin Science

Understanding the biological foundation of healthy hair

← Back to Education

What is Hair Made Of?

Hair is primarily composed of a protein called keratin, which is one of the toughest and most resilient biological materials found in nature. This protein is not unique to hair—it's also found in your skin, nails, and the outer layer of your teeth. However, hair contains keratin in its most concentrated and organized form.

Keratin molecules carry negative electrical charges, which is crucial to understanding how hair behaves and how to care for it properly. This negative charge influences how hair strands interact with each other and with external products and treatments.

Key Fact: The negative charges on keratin molecules cause hair strands to repel each other naturally, which is why healthy hair has volume and doesn't clump together.

The Three Layers of Hair

Hair has a complex structure consisting of three distinct layers, each with its own function:

1. The Cuticle (Outer Layer)

The cuticle is the outermost protective layer of the hair shaft, composed of overlapping transparent cells that resemble roof tiles or fish scales. These cells are made primarily of keratin and are arranged in a specific pattern that determines hair shine and smoothness.

  • Function: Protects the inner layers from damage
  • Composition: Transparent keratin cells
  • Health indicator: Smooth cuticles = shiny, healthy hair
  • Damage signs: Raised or broken cuticles = dull, frizzy hair

When cuticles lie flat, light reflects evenly, creating shine. When they're raised or damaged, light scatters, making hair appear dull and damaged. This is why treatments often focus on smoothing and sealing the cuticle layer.

2. The Cortex (Middle Layer)

Beneath the cuticle lies the cortex, which makes up the bulk of the hair shaft and contains most of the hair's strength, elasticity, and color.

  • Composition: Long polypeptide chains of keratin protein
  • Structure: Keratin filaments held together by chemical bonds
  • Color: Contains melanin (pigment) that gives hair its natural color
  • Strength: Responsible for hair's tensile strength and elasticity

The cortex determines your hair's texture, thickness, and how it holds styles. The more densely packed the keratin in the cortex, the stronger and thicker the hair.

3. The Medulla (Inner Core)

The innermost layer is the medulla, which doesn't exist in fine hair and is less developed in blonde or light hair.

  • Composition: Loosely packed keratin and air spaces
  • Function: Provides additional strength and texture
  • Presence: More prominent in coarse, dark hair

Keratin Bonds and Structure

The strength and behavior of hair depend on several types of bonds that hold keratin molecules together:

1. Peptide Bonds

Peptide bonds link amino acids together to form the long chains of the keratin protein. These are the strongest bonds in hair and cannot be broken by water, heat, or typical styling. They're only broken through chemical processes like relaxers or permanent waves.

2. Hydrogen Bonds

Hydrogen bonds form between amino acids within and between keratin chains. These bonds are temporary and can be broken and reformed by moisture and heat. This is why:

  • Hair becomes more pliable when wet
  • Hair can be styled with heat
  • Humidity affects hair texture
  • Setting hair with cool air locks in styles

3. Disulfide Bonds

Disulfide bonds form between cysteine amino acids in keratin and are responsible for hair's natural curl or wave pattern. These bonds are stronger than hydrogen bonds but weaker than peptide bonds. They can be:

  • Temporarily altered by perming and relaxing
  • Permanently changed by certain chemical treatments
  • Influenced by moisture and pH
Understanding Keratin Structure: The temporary nature of hydrogen bonds is why hair changes with weather, water, and humidity. The permanence of disulfide bonds is why genetic hair texture can't be changed without chemical treatment.

Electrical Charge and Hair Behavior

The negative electrical charges on keratin molecules are fundamental to hair behavior and styling:

Why Negative Charge Matters

  • Repulsion: Hair strands repel each other, maintaining separation and volume
  • Frizz: When the cuticle is raised, negative charges become exposed, causing frizz
  • Static: Dry conditions reduce conductivity, allowing static charge to build
  • Product interaction: Negatively charged products repel hair, not coating it

Positive Ions in Hair Care

This is why products often contain positively charged (cationic) ingredients like:

  • Silicones
  • Quaternary ammonium compounds
  • Protein treatments

Positive ions are attracted to the negative charges on hair, coating and smoothing the cuticle, reducing frizz and adding shine.

Nutritional Requirements for Keratin Production

Your body continuously produces new keratin in hair follicles. To maintain this production, you need:

Essential Nutrients

  • Protein: The building block of keratin (amino acids)
  • Sulfur: Needed for disulfide bond formation
  • Zinc: Required for protein synthesis
  • Iron: Needed for oxygen transport to hair follicles
  • B-vitamins: Support protein metabolism
  • Vitamin C: Needed for collagen and keratin cross-linking

pH and Hair Health

Hair has a natural pH of about 4.5-5.5 (acidic). Maintaining this pH is important because:

  • Acidic pH: Smooths the cuticle, adds shine, and strengthens bonds
  • Alkaline pH: Raises the cuticle, causes frizz, and weakens protein structure
  • Balance: Using pH-balanced products helps maintain hair integrity
Practical Tip: After shampooing with alkaline cleansers, use an acidic rinse (apple cider vinegar or conditioner) to close the cuticle and restore shine.

Hair Damage: Breaking the Bonds

Understanding keratin structure explains common hair damage:

  • Heat damage: Breaks hydrogen bonds and weakens protein structure
  • Chemical damage: Breaks disulfide and peptide bonds
  • UV damage: Breaks peptide bonds and darkens color
  • Mechanical damage: Breaks cuticle and cortex structure
  • Moisture loss: Disrupts hydrogen bonding and causes brittleness

Maintaining Healthy Keratin

To keep your keratin strong and healthy:

  • Minimize heat exposure
  • Use pH-balanced products
  • Keep hair moisturized
  • Eat protein-rich foods
  • Protect hair from UV damage
  • Avoid harsh chemicals
  • Get regular trims
  • Sleep on a silk pillowcase

Understanding your hair's keratin structure is the first step to effective hair care and maintenance.
